Section 1

WIDA Foundations 🌍

WIDA 基础：每位教师需要了解的语言发展框架

WIDA is a research consortium housed at the University of Wisconsin–Madison. It produces the most widely used English Language Development (ELD) standards and assessments in the world — used by U.S. states and by over 500 international schools, including schools across Asia. When educators talk about a student being a "Level 2" or "Level 4," they are usually speaking WIDA.

The current framework is the WIDA ELD Standards Framework, 2020 Edition. Its core message for content teachers is simple: language and content are taught together, not separately. A science teacher teaching plate tectonics is also teaching the language of explanation. An English teacher teaching theme is also teaching the language of argument.

The Can Do Philosophy

WIDA's foundation is asset-based thinking. Multilingual learners are not "students who can't speak English" — they are students who bring linguistic and cultural resources and who can do meaningful academic work at every level of English proficiency. Our job is to find what they can do today and build the next step.

MLWS connection: Our students read English at roughly Grades 3–9 but think at a true high-school level. The Can Do Philosophy says: never lower the thinking; scaffold the language. 我们的学生阅读水平有限，但思维能力是高中水平——降低语言难度，不降低思维要求。

The Four Big Ideas (2020 Edition)

Big IdeaWhat it means for your classroom
Equity of opportunity and access 机会公平Multilingual learners get full access to grade-level content — scaffolded, never watered down.
Integration of content and language 内容与语言融合Language is taught through subject content, not as a separate grammar class.
Collaboration among stakeholders 协作Content teachers, ESL specialists, and families plan together. Language is everyone's job.
A functional approach to language 功能性语言观We teach what language does (explain, argue, narrate, inform), not just vocabulary lists and grammar rules.

The Five ELD Standards Statements

These have stayed the same since the original framework. Standard 1 belongs to every adult in the building; Standards 2–5 connect language to the content areas.

1 · Social & Instructional Language 社交与课堂语言 2 · Language for Language Arts 语文学科语言 3 · Language for Mathematics 数学学科语言 4 · Language for Science 科学学科语言 5 · Language for Social Studies 社会学科语言

The Four Language Domains

Every WIDA level is measured across four domains. A student is rarely at the same level in all four — many MLWS students are stronger in reading than speaking.

👂 Listening 听 🗣 Speaking 说 📖 Reading 读 ✍️ Writing 写
Receptive vs. expressive: Listening and reading are interpretive (taking language in). Speaking and writing are expressive (producing language). Students' interpretive skills usually run ahead of their expressive skills — they understand more than they can produce. Plan output supports accordingly.

The Four Key Language Uses (KLUs)

The 2020 Edition organizes academic language across all disciplines into four high-frequency "genre families." If you know which KLU your lesson demands, you know which language to scaffold.

KLUStudents use language to…Classroom example
Narrate 叙述share and order experiences or stories, real or imaginedRetell the plot of Holes; describe a lab procedure step by step
Inform 说明define, describe, classify, and report factual informationReport features of a biome; define a vocabulary term in a Frayer Model
Explain 解释account for how things work or why things happenExplain why the central idea is supported by a detail; explain a math solution
Argue 论证justify claims with evidence and reasoningArgue which character changed most, citing the text

Section 2

Proficiency Levels in Practice 📈

六级语言水平：了解学生的现状，规划下一步

WIDA describes English development as a continuum of six levels. These levels align to the ACCESS for ELLs assessment, and the 2020 Edition's Proficiency Level Descriptors (PLDs) describe what student language looks like at each level across three dimensions: discourse (whole text), sentence, and word/phrase.

The Six Levels

LevelNameWhat you'll typically seeWhat helps most
1Entering 入门Single words, short memorized phrases; relies heavily on visuals and first language (L1)Visuals, realia, gestures, word banks, L1 support, yes/no and pointing responses
2Emerging 萌芽Short phrases and simple sentences on familiar topics; emerging academic vocabularySentence frames, matching tasks, labeled diagrams, partner support
3Developing 发展Connected simple sentences; expanding vocabulary; errors common but meaning clearSentence starters, graphic organizers, modeled paragraphs, word walls
4Expanding 扩展Multi-sentence connected discourse; uses some specific academic languageParagraph frames, academic discussion protocols, targeted feedback on form
5Bridging 衔接Extended, organized discourse approaching grade-level peers; technical language emergingComplex text work with annotation, register and genre coaching
6Reaching 达标Language comparable to proficient English-speaking peers for grade levelGrade-level rigor with normal monitoring
Common mistake: Treating a level as a label for the student instead of a description of their current language. Levels change. A student can be Level 4 in reading and Level 2 in speaking at the same time. Always think domain by domain. 等级描述的是语言现状，不是学生能力的标签。

Can Do Descriptors

The Can Do Descriptors are WIDA's most teacher-friendly tool: grade-cluster charts listing what learners can do at each level in each domain. They were built for the earlier framework but remain officially relevant and in use alongside the 2020 Edition. Use them to:

  • Set realistic output expectations for a task ("At Level 2, a compare/contrast answer might be a completed Venn diagram with word-bank words — not a paragraph.")
  • Choose scaffolds that target the next level up, not the current one
  • Write differentiated success criteria for the same lesson objective

Same Objective, Different Language Expectations — a worked example

Objective (Grade 11 English): SWBAT identify the central idea of a non-fiction passage IOT evaluate how supporting details develop it.
Levels 1–2Choose the central idea from 3 picture-supported options; sort 4 details as "supports / does not support" using a word bank.
Levels 3–4State the central idea using a sentence frame ("The central idea is ___ because ___") and explain two supporting details with a starter.
Levels 5–6Write a short paragraph evaluating which detail develops the idea most effectively, citing the text.
Note the thinking — identify and evaluate — is identical in all three columns. Only the language demand changes.

Scaffold Up, Not Down

The PLD continuum is a planning tool: look at what the student does now, then provide supports that pull language toward the next level. Supports are temporary by design — as proficiency grows, scaffolds fade. A Level 3 student given Level 1 supports forever will stay comfortable and stuck; a Level 3 student given Level 4-targeted frames will grow.

One-sentence takeaway: Know each student's level by domain, hold the cognitive bar steady, and aim every scaffold one level above where the student is now. ✅ You have completed Section 2. Continue to Section 3: The Ellevation Framework.

The Ellevation Framework 🧭

Ellevation：将研究转化为可直接使用的课堂策略

Ellevation is a U.S. English Learner platform serving districts that educate over half of America's EL students. Its Ellevation Strategies product packages research-based instruction into a clear hierarchy that any teacher — not just ESL specialists — can pick up and use. Its motto matches WIDA's: all teachers are teachers of language.

The Hierarchy: Practices → Strategies → Activities

LayerWhat it isExample
Practice 实践A broad, research-backed principle of language teaching"Promote structured oral output"
Strategy 策略A repeatable approach inside a practice"Use academic conversation protocols"
Activity 活动A concrete, classroom-ready routine with materials, steps, and level adaptationsWhich Corner?, Directed Discourse

Every Ellevation activity ships with content-area examples (ELA, math, science, social studies), downloadable materials such as graphic organizers, posters, and sentence starters, and tips for adapting to different proficiency levels — which is exactly the structure our Strategy Library in Section 4 follows.

The Core Practices Every MLWS Teacher Should Know

1 · Make Input Comprehensible 让输入可理解

Based on Stephen Krashen's theory of second language acquisition: students acquire language when they understand most but not all of what they hear and read — input pitched just beyond their current level (think Vygotsky's zone of proximal development). Practical moves: adapted speech (slower rate, clear enunciation, fewer idioms and run-ons), visuals and realia, modeling and demonstrations, and chunked text.

2 · Structure Oral Output 构建口语输出

Students don't acquire academic English by listening alone — they must produce it. But "discuss with your partner" fails ELs without structure. Effective oral output is planned, supported, and accountable: assigned partners, sentence frames, roles, and protocols like Directed Discourse in which students hold authentic academic conversations with peers, or Which Corner? where students physically move to a category corner and justify their choice.

3 · Teach Academic Vocabulary Deliberately 系统教学术语

Academic words don't stick from one exposure. High-impact vocabulary teaching uses multiple encounters in multiple modes: student-friendly definitions, visuals, examples and non-examples (the Frayer Model), word walls students actually use, and structured chances to say and write the word the same day they learn it.

4 · Activate and Build Background Knowledge 激活并建立背景知识

Comprehension rides on what the reader already knows. Many ELs have the knowledge but in their first language; others are missing culturally specific context (a Thanksgiving reference, a baseball metaphor). Anticipation guides, picture previews, brief videos, and L1 brainstorming connect new learning to what students bring.

5 · Scaffold Reading and Writing 搭建读写支架

Grade-level text stays; the route into it changes. Graphic organizers, cloze passages, paragraph frames, annotation routines, and guided notes give ELs a path through complex text and a structure for producing academic writing.

How Ellevation + WIDA fit together: WIDA tells you where the student is (level, domain) and what the language goal is (Key Language Use, Language Expectation). Ellevation-style strategies tell you what to do Monday morning — the concrete activity, adapted to the student's level. Data → goal → strategy. WIDA 提供定位与目标，策略提供具体做法。
One-sentence takeaway: Pick the practice that matches your lesson's weak point — input, output, vocabulary, background, or literacy — then choose one concrete activity and adapt it by level. ✅ You have completed Section 3. Continue to Section 4: the Strategy Library.
