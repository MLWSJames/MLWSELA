Welcome: When Words Are the Barrier

欢迎：当词汇成为障碍

Picture a Grade 11 student at MLWS. She can analyze a plot twist in Chinese with the sophistication of any high schooler — but hand her an English passage and she stalls on the second sentence, decoding word by word while her classmates move on to discussion. Her thinking is not the problem. The words are the barrier.

This is the exact gap Catherine Gibbons describes in her 2026 Edutopia article on structured literacy for older struggling readers: by secondary school, instruction assumes students have finished learning to read and are now reading to learn — yet many still need targeted phonics, word-pattern, and morphology instruction. The materials that teach those skills are almost always written for young children, and older students find them tedious or insulting.

Our MLWS learners live in this gap every day: high analytical capability, emerging English decoding. This module gives you a practical, research-informed approach — and shows you how to use AI to build age-respectful, level-appropriate texts in minutes instead of hours.

Assessment First, Comprehension Always

先评估，始终以理解为核心

Data Drives Instruction

The approach begins with assessment — not to label students, but to find the specific gap. Gibbons recommends combining an informal reading inventory with a spelling inventory to gather evidence across four areas:

AreaWhat it tells youMLWS example
DecodingCan the student turn letters into sounds accurately?Student reads "grew" as "grow" — vowel team confusion
FluencyIs reading smooth enough to leave brainpower for meaning?Word-by-word reading in Grade 11 texts
ComprehensionDoes the student understand what was read?Can retell in Chinese but not locate evidence in English
SpellingWhich patterns are mapped, which are guessed?Writes "moon" correctly but "floo" for "flew"

A finding like "this student struggles with vowel teams and has no solid grasp of morphology" is actionable. "This student is a weak reader" is not.

MLWS connection: Our ARC placement data plays exactly this role — it tells us the band, but the informal inventories tell us the pattern to target within that band.

The Non-Negotiable: Comprehension Stays at the Heart

Here is the part that separates this approach from drill-and-kill phonics packets: even when foundational skills are the target, comprehension remains the center of instruction. Older students must spend time engaging meaningfully with text — making connections, forming opinions, thinking critically — or the word work becomes purposeless in their eyes and ours.

Rule of thumb: Word patterns are taught in service of meaning-making, never instead of it. The pattern gets students into the text; the thinking is why they stay.

Section 3 of 6

The Four-Step Targeted Lesson

四步定向词汇模式教学

Once assessment identifies a target pattern (for example, the long /u/ spellings ue, ew, ou, oo), the lesson follows a gradual-release arc. Notice how responsibility shifts from teacher to student across the four steps — and how the lesson ends in real reading, not in a worksheet.

StepWhat happensWho is doing the thinking
1 · Explicit InstructionTeacher names the pattern directly and anchors it in known words: "Think about words you have seen, like blue, grew, group, moon. What do these patterns have in common?"Teacher models; students notice
2 · Guided PracticeStudents sort words (true, few, soup, school) into pattern categories — and must explain why each spelling is used. The justification is the learning.Shared
3 · ApplicationStudents complete sentences with the target pattern: "The flowers began to bl__m in the spring." Then they justify: "I chose oo because I have seen it in moon and food."Mostly student
4 · Contextual ReadingStudents read a short, high-interest passage where the pattern appears naturally. Comprehension comes first (main idea, agree/disagree with the author) — then students return to highlight and code the pattern words.Student
Why the order inside Step 4 matters: Meaning first, pattern-hunting second. Reversing it turns reading into a scavenger hunt and kills comprehension. Doing it in this order builds orthographic mapping — the brain's permanent link between spelling, sound, and meaning — which is what ultimately improves both fluency and spelling.
Measuring growth: Use formative checks in targeted groups plus quick biweekly teacher-made pattern assessments. Watch student writing too — when a student who used to confuse ew and oo starts spelling them correctly in a journal, transfer has happened.

Section 4 of 6

Leveraging AI for Leveled, Decodable, Age-Respectful Texts

用人工智能生成分级文本

The Problem AI Actually Solves

The hardest part of Step 4 has always been the text itself: it must be decodable at the student's level, rich in the target pattern, and interesting to a teenager. Commercially, such texts barely exist. Writing them by hand takes hours. AI closes this gap: you can request a passage with a specific reading level, spelling pattern, topic, word count, and question set — tailored to your exact group. Gibbons reports the full cycle (prompt → review → revise) takes about 20–30 minutes, depending on prompt quality.

Anatomy of a Strong Prompt

Gibbons' sample prompt asks for a highly engaging passage of about 650 words targeting the long o pattern, written at a third-grade reading level, appealing to middle/high school struggling readers, with three inferential questions at the end. Break that down and you find five load-bearing components:

ComponentWhy it matters
1 · Target pattern ("the long o pattern")Guarantees the passage is decodable practice, not just easy reading
2 · Reading level ("third-grade reading level")Matches assessed decoding ability — the whole point of leveling
3 · Audience ("middle/high school struggling readers")Keeps content age-respectful — a Grade 3 level does NOT mean Grade 3 topics
4 · Length ("about 650 words")Controls cognitive load and lesson timing
5 · Comprehension task ("three inferential thinking questions")Keeps comprehension at the heart — the text arrives ready for meaning-making
Re-prompting is normal. If the output misses the mark ("This passage is actually higher than third-grade level — please lower it"), say exactly what is wrong and what you want changed. Precision in, precision out.
MLWS bonus move: For our learners, you can add a component Gibbons doesn't need: "Include a short English–Chinese glossary of 5 key words." Leveled decoding practice plus L1 support in one prompt.

Professional Judgment: The Review Checklist

AI output is a draft, never a handout. Gibbons is emphatic: generated material must be reviewed and edited — checked for spelling, readability, appropriate content, and clarity — before any student sees it. Why This Is Worth It: Automaticity → Higher-Order Thinking

Every word a student decodes automatically is cognitive capacity freed for analysis, inference, and argument — the things our students are already capable of in Chinese. When words stop being the barrier, older struggling readers approach texts with confidence, and reflection on their own growth turns them from passive readers into active readers. That confidence transfers across content areas and beyond school.
