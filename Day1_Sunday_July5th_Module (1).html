Lesson 1: History and Prehistory

What is History?

History is the study of the past using written records. When humans invented writing about 5,000 years ago, they created a new way to remember events, laws, trade, and stories. Any period before writing is called prehistory.

Key Idea: The dividing line between history and prehistory is the invention of writing. People in prehistory were just as intelligent as people today.

Sources of Evidence

Historians use primary sources (documents from the time) and secondary sources (later interpretations). Archaeologists study prehistory using artifacts (objects made by humans), fossils (remains of living things), and features (non-movable structures).

Lesson 2: Homo Sapiens and Early Migration

The Emergence of Homo Sapiens

Homo sapiens (modern humans) emerged in Africa around 300,000 years ago. The oldest known fossils were found at Jebel Irhoud, Morocco, dated to about 315,000 years ago.

Key Idea: Homo sapiens did not replace other species through conquest. They likely out-reproduced earlier hominins through social and technological advantages.

Out of Africa

Between 120,000 and 50,000 years ago, Homo sapiens began leaving Africa. They reached the Near East by 60,000 years ago, Europe by 45,000 years ago, and Australia by 50,000 years ago.

Lesson 3: Paleolithic Technology, Culture and Art

The Paleolithic Era

The Paleolithic (Old Stone Age) spans from about 2.6 million years ago to roughly 10,000 BCE. Humans lived as hunter-gatherers, moving from place to place to find food.

Key Idea: The Paleolithic is divided into Lower, Middle, and Upper periods. Each shows advances in tool technology and social complexity.

Stone Tool Technology

Oldowan tools (2.6 mya) were simple choppers. Acheulean tools (1.7 mya) included hand axes. Mousterian tools (300,000 ya) used prepared cores. Upper Paleolithic tools included blades and bone needles.

Lesson 4: The Neolithic Revolution and Early Agriculture

What Was the Neolithic Revolution?

The Neolithic Revolution (about 10,000 BCE) marks the shift from hunting and gathering to farming and herding. It changed where people lived, what they ate, and how they organized society.

Key Idea: The revolution was not a single event. It happened independently in the Fertile Crescent, China, Mesoamerica, the Andes, and sub-Saharan Africa.

Causes of the Revolution

The end of the Ice Age brought warmer weather. Growing populations needed more reliable food. The extinction of large game reduced hunting success. Humans gradually began planting useful wild species.

Lesson 5: The Dawn of Agriculture

Domestication

Domestication is the process of changing wild plants and animals to make them more useful to humans. Over generations, farmers selected plants with larger seeds and stronger stalks.

Key Idea: Domestication was a mutual relationship. Plants and animals gained protection. Humans gained food and labor.

First Domesticated Species

In the Fertile Crescent: wheat, barley, peas, goats, sheep, pigs, cattle. In East Asia: rice, millet. In Mesoamerica: maize (corn) from teosinte.
